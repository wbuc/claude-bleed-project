# Font Pairings Research for iOS Mobile App (Bleed)

**Date:** 2026-03-07
**Purpose:** Premium, clean, modern font pairings for a health/wellness iOS app

---

## Summary

15 font pairings researched across Google Fonts, Fontshare (free), Apple system fonts, and paid options. Organized by category with specific recommendations for a health/wellness iOS app like Bleed.

---

## Pairing 1: SF Pro Display + New York (Apple System Fonts)

| Role | Font |
|------|------|
| **Heading** | SF Pro Display |
| **Body** | New York (Apple's serif) |

**Why they work together:** Apple designed New York as a direct companion to San Francisco. The serif/sans-serif contrast creates clear hierarchy, and both fonts share optical sizing that adapts automatically at different sizes. SF Pro Display handles headings at 20pt+, while New York provides warm, readable body text with a premium editorial feel.

**Best for:** Native iOS apps that want to feel "at home" on Apple devices. Health, editorial, and premium lifestyle apps.

**Cost:** FREE -- included in iOS. No need to bundle fonts. Renders natively with zero performance overhead.

**Mobile advantage:** Both support Dynamic Type out of the box. Variable optical sizes adjust automatically. Lightest possible font load since the OS already has them.

## Pairing 2: SF Pro + SF Pro Rounded

| Role | Font |
|------|------|
| **Heading** | SF Pro Display |
| **Body** | SF Pro Rounded |

**Why they work together:** Same family, different personality. SF Pro Display gives sharp, authoritative headings while SF Pro Rounded softens the body text with warm, approachable curves. The rounded variant adds friendliness without sacrificing Apple's design language.

**Best for:** Health/wellness apps, fitness trackers, any app that wants to feel approachable but polished. Apple uses this combination in Health and Fitness+ apps.

**Cost:** FREE -- system fonts, zero bundle size.

**Mobile advantage:** Native rendering. Supports all iOS accessibility features including Dynamic Type and Bold Text.

## Pairing 3: DM Serif Display + DM Sans

| Role | Font |
|------|------|
| **Heading** | DM Serif Display |
| **Body** | DM Sans |

**Why they work together:** Designed as a family by Colophon Foundry for Google Fonts. DM Serif Display has high-contrast transitional serifs with delicate detailing for headlines. DM Sans is a low-contrast geometric sans optimized for small text. The shared design DNA creates visual harmony while the serif/sans contrast drives hierarchy.

**Best for:** Premium health/wellness apps, editorial-style interfaces, apps that want to feel elevated and sophisticated. The elegance of the serif heading signals quality, while DM Sans keeps UI text clean and scannable.

**Cost:** FREE -- Google Fonts (open source, SIL Open Font License).

**Mobile advantage:** DM Sans was specifically optimized for small-size rendering on screens. Both are lightweight.

## Pairing 4: Fraunces + Inter

| Role | Font |
|------|------|
| **Heading** | Fraunces |
| **Body** | Inter |

**Why they work together:** Fraunces is a quirky "Old Style" soft-serif with retro-modern character -- its wonky, organic shapes bring warmth and personality. Inter is the workhorse of screen typography, designed specifically for digital UI readability. Together they create what designers call "retro-modern editorial meets functional digital clarity."

**Best for:** Brand-forward health/wellness apps that want to stand out. The Fraunces headings give distinct personality (think premium DTC brand), while Inter handles all the functional UI work flawlessly.

**Cost:** FREE -- both on Google Fonts. Fraunces is a variable font with axes for weight, softness, wonk, and optical size (9 weights, 3 optical sizes).

**Mobile advantage:** Inter was built from the ground up for screens. Fraunces as a variable font keeps file size manageable while offering extensive style range.

## Pairing 5: Mulish + Karla

| Role | Font |
|------|------|
| **Heading** | Mulish |
| **Body** | Karla |

**Why they work together:** Both are clean, minimal sans-serifs but with complementary personalities. Mulish is geometric and composed; Karla is grotesque-style with a humanist warmth. Together they feel "adaptable and calm" -- exactly the emotional register a health app needs. The subtle contrast (geometric vs humanist) creates hierarchy without visual noise.

**Best for:** Wellness apps, health tech, meditation apps, clean SaaS. Specifically cited by multiple design sources as ideal for health/wellness contexts.

**Cost:** FREE -- Google Fonts.

**Mobile advantage:** Both render cleanly at small sizes. Karla's generous spacing prevents cramping on mobile screens.

## Pairing 6: Satoshi + General Sans

| Role | Font |
|------|------|
| **Heading** | Satoshi |
| **Body** | General Sans |

**Why they work together:** Both are from Fontshare (Indian Type Foundry) and share a contemporary geometric DNA. Satoshi has a slightly more pronounced geometric character with stark contrast for headings, while General Sans is more compact and rational for body text -- making it excellent for content-dense mobile screens. The effect is clean, precise, and unmistakably modern.

**Best for:** Premium tech-forward apps, modern health/wellness apps, fintech, productivity tools. This is the "startup premium" look that brands like Linear and Vercel popularized.

**Cost:** FREE -- Fontshare (free for personal and commercial use).

**Mobile advantage:** General Sans has slightly extended characters and compact proportions designed for dense mobile UIs. Satoshi maintains clarity at small sizes with near curves and open forms.

## Pairing 7: Clash Display + Satoshi

| Role | Font |
|------|------|
| **Heading** | Clash Display |
| **Body** | Satoshi |

**Why they work together:** Clash Display is a neo-grotesk with a distinctive twist -- its nearly closed apertures (like the tight 'c') make it eye-catching and editorial for headlines. Satoshi provides the clean, geometric precision needed for readable body text. The contrast between Clash Display's personality and Satoshi's neutrality creates strong visual hierarchy.

**Best for:** Premium brand-forward apps, lifestyle/wellness brands that want bold headlines, apps going for a magazine-editorial aesthetic on mobile.

**Cost:** FREE -- both on Fontshare.

**Mobile advantage:** Clash Display performs best at larger display sizes (headings), while Satoshi handles small text well. This natural size separation maps perfectly to mobile heading/body hierarchy.

**Cost:** FREE -- Google Fonts.

| **Body** | Raleway |

| Role | Font |

**Cost:** FREE -- both on Google Fonts.

**Mobile advantage:** Lexend's research-backed letterform design specifically improves readability on screens. Outstanding choice if accessibility is a priority.

## Top Recommendations for Bleed (Health/Wellness Period Tracker)

Based on this research, the strongest candidates for a premium, privacy-first period tracking app:

### Tier 1 -- Best Fit
1. **DM Serif Display + DM Sans** -- Elegant headers signal premium quality; clean body text for daily use. Feels elevated without being cold.
2. **SF Pro + New York** -- Zero overhead, native feel, supports all iOS accessibility features. The serif body text adds warmth that pure sans-serif apps lack.

## Typography Best Practices for Mobile (Key Findings)

- **Base font size:** 16px minimum for body text on mobile
- **Line height:** 1.5-2x font size for body text; 1-1.25x for display text
- **Limit to 1-2 typefaces** per app (more indicates poor design discipline)
- **Variable fonts** reduce load times by bundling multiple styles in one file
- **Test on iPhone SE first**, then scale up -- if it works on the smallest screen, it works everywhere
- **Prefer fonts with 5+ weights** for flexible hierarchy (indicates a well-designed typeface)
- **Use SF Pro Display at 20pt+**, SF Pro Text below 20pt (Apple's optical sizing breakpoint)
- **High x-height** = better mobile readability (Manrope, Inter, DM Sans all excel here)
